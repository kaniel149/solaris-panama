Roof Types in Panama

Panama's tropical climate means specific roof materials dominate. Each type has different considerations for solar mounting.

Galvanized Metal

★★★★★

Most common in Panama. Excellent for solar — lightweight clamps attach directly to standing seams or through-bolt to purlins. Fast installation, no roof penetration needed for standing seam.

Standing Seam Metal

★★★★★

Premium metal roofing. S-5! or similar clamps grip the seam — zero penetrations. Ideal for solar. Common on commercial buildings and upscale residential.

Concrete / Flat Roof

★★★★☆

Common in commercial and modern residential. Requires tilt mounting frames (5-10°). Chemical anchor bolts used. Must verify waterproofing membrane integrity.

Clay / Concrete Tile

★★★☆☆

Found in traditional and Spanish-style homes. Requires tile hooks and careful handling — tiles can crack. More labor-intensive. Structural assessment critical.

Panel Orientation in the Tropics

Panama sits at ~8°N latitude — nearly on the equator. This changes the optimal panel orientation compared to northern or southern hemisphere installations.

N S E W
E ↔ W BEST

Near the equator, the sun passes almost directly overhead. East-West orientation captures morning and afternoon sun evenly.

  • Near-flat is ideal: 5-10° tilt is optimal for Panama's latitude. Steeper roofs lose production.
  • East-West split: Spreading panels E-W on flat roofs gives more consistent generation throughout the day.
  • North or South facing: Both work well at 8°N — the difference is only 2-3% annually. Don't overthink it.
  • Avoid pure vertical walls: BIPV on walls loses 40%+ production at this latitude.
  • Rain drainage: Minimum 5° tilt recommended so tropical rain cleans panels naturally.

Shading Analysis

Even partial shading can significantly reduce solar output. In Panama, common shading sources include tropical trees, water tanks, and neighboring structures.

Common Shading Sources in Panama

  • Mango & almond trees: Large canopy, often close to houses. May need trimming or removal.
  • Palm trees: Thin trunk but tall — can cast long morning/evening shadows.
  • Neighboring buildings: Especially in urban areas. Check shadow patterns at 9 AM, noon, and 3 PM.
  • Water tanks (tinaco): Roof-mounted tanks are everywhere in Panama. May need relocation.
  • Satellite dishes & antennas: Small but close — can shade 1-2 panels significantly.
  • Power lines: Thin but cast moving shadows. Usually not critical unless directly over the roof.

Time-of-Day Shadow Check

☀
Morning (7-9 AM)

Check for objects to the east — trees, hills, buildings casting long westward shadows.

☀
Midday (11 AM-1 PM)

Sun is nearly overhead. Only very close, tall objects cast shadows. Best production window.

☀
Afternoon (3-5 PM)

Check for objects to the west. Afternoon production is important for peak-demand offset.

Structural Assessment Checklist

A solar array adds 12-15 kg/m² of load. Most Panama roofs handle this easily, but verification is essential for safety and permitting.

  • Roof age: If galvanized metal is >15 years, check for rust and thinning. May need replacement before solar.
  • Purlin spacing: Standard 1.2m spacing supports panel clamps. Wider spacing may need additional rails.
  • Structural integrity: No sagging beams, cracked concrete, or rotting wood supports.
  • Wind load: Panama's Caribbean coast gets tropical storms. Verify mounting can handle 160 km/h winds per ASEP code.
  • Waterproofing: For concrete/flat roofs, verify membrane condition. Penetrations must be sealed with proper flashing.
  • Access: Ensure at least 1m walkway around array perimeter for maintenance and fire safety.
  • Electrical pathway: Route from roof to main electrical panel. Conduit path, junction box location, breaker space.
  • Ground mount option: If roof is unsuitable, ground-mounted systems are viable where land is available.

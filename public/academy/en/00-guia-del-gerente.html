Manager's Guide — Solaris Panama Sales Academy

Audience: Kaniel (the Israeli manager) + any future sales manager
Language: Hebrew — this document is not intended for local salespeople
Position in the organization: Internal management document only

1. The Recruitment Profile — Who Is the Right Salesperson for Azuero

What to Look For

A good Solaris Panama salesperson is not necessarily the most charismatic person in the room. They are the one most connected to the community.

Mandatory criteria:

  • Native Spanish (basic Spanish is not enough — the Azuero population speaks quickly with a local dialect)
  • Some connection to the Azuero area — grew up there, has family there, knows business owners
  • Previous sales experience (doesn't have to be solar — insurance, vehicles, real estate are all excellent)
  • Holds a driver's license and a vehicle (field visits are essential)
  • Willing to work during hours that suit clients: early morning (8-10) and afternoon (14-16)

Significant advantages but not mandatory:

  • Basic English (for expat hotel owners)
  • Personal connections with hotel owners, farms, or retail managers
  • Previous experience with Law 417 or ASEP

Red flags in recruitment:

  • "I'm a good salesperson, but I didn't know the area" — in an environment like Azuero, this is a critical problem
  • Sales experience only in a cold-call center environment — the culture is completely different
  • No accountability for goals — "if the customer didn't buy, it's not my fault"
  • Expects to close deals in the first meeting — the Panamanian pace doesn't work that way

Recommended Recruitment Process

  1. Initial interview: 20 min on WhatsApp — understand the background and familiarity with the area.
  2. Role-play test: Have them face a "customer" with a price objection — without advance preparation.
  3. Reference check: Ask specifically: "Give me the name of a customer from a previous job that I can call."
  4. Two-week trial: Not hired — working. Send them to Azuero for a week, see if they come back with connections.

2. How to Run Each Module

The academy is structured for 10 modules over 30 days. Here are the management principles:

General Principle

Every module is a conversation, not a lecture. The manager doesn't read slides — they ask questions, the salesperson responds, then you practice.

Recommended Format for Each Module Session (90-120 minutes)

TimeActivity
0-15 minThe salesperson summarizes what they read — the manager listens and fills in gaps
15-45 minHands-on practice: role-play of a scenario from the module
45-60 minDiscussion of errors seen in the field (if any already exist)
60-75 minQuiz + review together
75-90 minPreparation for the next session — what to read, what to practice

Modules That Require Extra Time

  • Module 7 (Objections): Practice at least 7 of the objections in role-play before moving on. This is the heart of the work.
  • Module 9 (CRM + follow-up): Go into the CRM together and make sure they understand all the statuses in practice.
  • Module 10 (Simulations): This is not one session — plan 3 role-play sessions on 3 different days.

3. How to Run a Role-Play and Rate It

Rules of the Game

  1. Don't interrupt during the role-play. Even if they make a mistake — continue to the end. Stopping kills the real feeling.
  2. The manager plays the client. Be a moderate client — not too easy and not impossible. A client who is too easy teaches nothing.
  3. After the role-play — 3 minutes of feedback. The order: what worked well, one thing to change, practice again the specific moment that failed.

Rating Scale (1-5)

ScoreMeaning
5Exceeds expectations — did something not taught but it worked
4Full execution with confidence
3Passed — did what was required, even if not smoothly
2Key parts were missing — need to go back and practice
1Failed — lacks basic understanding, needs to review the material

What Not to Do During Coaching

  • Don't laugh if they make a mistake — it discourages rather than encourages
  • Don't give feedback like "overall it was good, just..." — be specific
  • Don't skip role-play because "there's no time" — it's the most important thing you do

4. Weekly Pipeline Review Structure

Every Sunday (or Monday morning): 30-45 minute pipeline meeting.

Meeting Structure

Part 1 (10 minutes) — What closed this week?

  • How many contracts were signed?
  • How many proposals were sent?
  • How many visits were made?

Part 2 (15 minutes) — What is about to close?

  • Leads in "proposal sent" stage for over 7 days — what is the status?
  • Leads in "negotiation" — what's missing to close?
  • Plan together the 3 critical actions for the week

Part 3 (10 minutes) — What is stalled?

  • Deals that haven't moved in over 3 weeks — should they be moved to nurture?
  • Leads without a next action — fix right now

Part 4 (10 minutes) — Skills improvement

  • A scenario that happened during the week that requires role-play (difficult client, new objection, a call that didn't go well)

7. Red Flags — A Representative Who Won't Survive

These are the signs that a person is not right for the role. You can see them in the first weeks:

Red Flags During Training

  • Doesn't learn the word-for-word versions of the messages (says "I'll say something similar") — that doesn't work. The scripts were written from experience.
  • Skips CRM updates ("I'll remember") — in an environment of 30+ leads this is a disaster.
  • Responds to criticism with defensiveness rather than curiosity — you can't teach someone who isn't willing to listen.

Red Flags in the Field

  • Doesn't show up for scheduled visits — in Panama this closes doors permanently.
  • Sends proposals without the manager's approval — even after being warned.
  • Promises clients prices, terms, or timelines that haven't been approved — will create legal problems.
  • Doesn't look for referrals — leaving large amounts of money on the table in a region where everyone knows everyone.

Red Flags in Reporting

  • Number of visits reported doesn't match what's in the CRM.
  • Comes unprepared to pipeline meetings.
  • Asks for more time to "warm up leads" without any change in approach — sometimes this is deflection from real difficulties.

What to do with a struggling representative?

  1. A direct, clear conversation — "I saw X, I expected Y, what happened?"
  2. A 2-week improvement plan with specific measurable goals.
  3. If there's no improvement — a quick and respectful separation. In a small area, how you part ways matters.

8. Updating the Scripts from the Field

The scripts written in the sales materials are good, but the field is the real boss.

Monthly Update Mechanism

At the end of every month, ask the representative:

  1. "What objection did you hear that wasn't in the material?"
  2. "What did you say that worked better than what's written?"
  3. "Is there a sentence a customer said repeatedly — what was it?"

What to do with the answers:

  • If a new objection came up 3 times — add it to Module 7.
  • If a different wording works better — update the script, don't delete, just add.
  • If there is a local culture factor that influences things (holiday, season, event) — document it and train on it.

Who Updates the Scripts?

The manager only. The representative brings observations, the manager decides what goes into the material. This prevents confusion.

9. Questions Worth Asking Yourself Every Month

  • Does my representative know their target? Not "to grow" — specific numbers.
  • Does the CRM reflect reality? Check 5 random leads every week.
  • Am I approving proposals in a reasonable time? If a client waits 3 days for approval — it's killing deals.
  • When did you last listen to a real call (not a role-play) from the representative? Do it at least once a month.
  • Do we have one case study with real photos and real numbers? That is the most powerful sales tool in the field.

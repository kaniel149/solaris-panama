Module 7: Objections — A Response for Every Concern, Empathy First

Level: Beginner–Intermediate | Estimated time: 120 minutes | Prerequisite: Modules 1–6

🎯 Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the real fear behind every objection (not just the surface complaint).
  2. Apply the LAER framework — Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond — in any situation.
  3. Respond to the 14 most common objections with word-for-word scripts.
  4. Handle 6 additional objections frequent in the Azuero market.
  5. Never discount the price — add value instead.

Theory: The LAER Framework

"An objection is not a 'no.' It is a request for more information with an emotion on top."

The LAER framework (Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond) is the core method for handling any objection. Always apply it in that exact order — never skip steps.

L — Listen

Let the prospect finish without interrupting. Don't prepare your response while they are talking: listen genuinely. The first 3–5 seconds after an objection are intentional silence — do not rush your response.

A — Acknowledge

Recognize the emotion before presenting data. The prospect needs to feel understood before they are willing to listen.

Valid phrases: *"I completely understand…"* / *"You're right to ask that…"* / *"It makes perfect sense to feel that way…"*

What you never say: *"But…"* / *"You're wrong…"* / *"No, that's not how it works."*

Note: saying "but" cancels everything you just said before it. Use "and also" or simply continue after a pause.

E — Explore

Ask ONE question to uncover the real fear. The objection you hear is rarely the underlying issue.

Useful phrases: *"What specifically concerns you about this?"* / *"Where in the process do you feel the most uncertainty?"*

R — Respond

Now respond — but to the real fear you uncovered, not to the surface complaint. Use data, stories, and options based on what you explored together. Always end with a micro-close: *"Does that give you more clarity to move forward?"*

The Playbook Objections: Word-for-Word Responses

Objection 1: "It's too expensive" / "The investment is too high"

Real fear: They haven't seen enough evidence of the return.

A — Acknowledge:

"I understand — it sounds like it's the upfront investment that's the concern, not the concept itself."

E — Explore:

"Is it the total amount that worries you, or is it more the uncertainty about whether you'll actually recover that investment?"

R — Respond:

"Let's look at your numbers together. How much do you pay for electricity per month? [Calculate together] With a [X] kW system, your payback is [Y] years. After that, it's 20+ years of essentially free electricity."

If they keep resisting: present the PPA ($0 upfront, pays per kWh at a rate lower than the utility).

Absolute rule: Never discount the price. Add value — extended warranty, priority monitoring, additional maintenance visits.

Objection 2: "Let me think about it"

Real fear: Something unspoken is blocking the decision.

A — Acknowledge:

"Of course."

E — Explore:

"Just so I can understand better — is there something specific that raises a question for you? That way I can give you the right information."
"Is it the cost, the process, or something else?"
"Do you need to consult with anyone else?" (If yes: identify whether they are the actual decision-maker or if there's someone else.)

R — Respond:

"Perfect. How about we talk on [SPECIFIC DAY] at [HORA]? That gives you time to review it at your own pace."

Rule: Always propose a specific date. "Whenever you can" generates no commitment.

Objection 3: "I don't trust solar technology"

Real fear: They heard something negative, or the technology feels experimental to them.

A — Acknowledge:

"You're right to be cautious — there are a lot of promises out there in the market."

R — Respond:

"The panels we use carry a 25-year manufacturer warranty. Panama already has more than 170 MW installed with 6,000+ users. This isn't experimental — it's proven."
"The AES solar plant in Las Tablas has been operating for years. This is the same technology, just on your roof."

Objection 4: "I tried solar before and it didn't work"

Real fear: A prior bad experience with an installer, quality, or expectations.

E — Explore first:

"I'm sorry to hear that. What exactly went wrong?"

R — Respond based on what they share:

  • If it was a bad installer: *"That's why we offer a full warranty and remote monitoring. If something fails, we know about it before you do."*
  • If the system was poorly sized: *"We do a real analysis of your consumption — we don't guess or use generic numbers."*
  • If there were paperwork problems: *"We handle all the paperwork with ASEP and the utility ourselves. You sign, we file."*

Objection 5: "I need to consult with my partner / wife / board of directors"

Real fear: Could be real, or could be an escape to avoid deciding today.

A — Acknowledge:

"Completely understandable."

R — Respond:

"Would it help if I prepared an executive summary with the numbers so they can review it together?"
"When do you meet? I can be available by phone if they have questions during the meeting."

Key move:

"Would it be useful if I presented directly to them? Sometimes it's easier than explaining it yourself."

Objection 6: "It's not the right time" / "Not right now"

Real fear: No urgency — the status quo is comfortable.

R — Respond with honest urgency:

"I understand. But here's something worth sharing: Law 417, which provides the tax exemption, has no confirmed expiration date. When the policy changes — and eventually it will — the cost goes up 15 to 20%."
"Also, every month that passes is $[MONTO_MENSUAL] you're paying the utility instead of your own roof. That cost doesn't stop."

Objection 7: "The panels get damaged by the weather here" (rain, heat, wind)

Real fear: Genuine concern about the tropical climate of Azuero.

A — Acknowledge:

"That's a very valid question, and it makes sense to ask it in Azuero."

R — Respond:

"The panels are designed to withstand hail, winds up to 200 km/h, and extreme temperatures. Panama has ideal conditions — rain actually cleans the panels naturally, and solar radiation is among the best in the world: 5–6 kWh/m²/day."
"In fact, Panama's moderate heat is better than desert climates where panels overheat and lose efficiency."

Objection 8: "What if I sell the property or move?"

Real fear: Concerned about having an investment "trapped" in a property.

R — Respond:

"A solar system increases your property value by 4–6%. If you sell, it's a real differentiator — the buyer inherits low electricity costs, and that raises the sale price."
"If you have a PPA, it can be transferred to the new owner. If you bought the system outright, it's an asset that's already paying for itself."

Objection 9: "I'd rather wait for the technology to improve"

Real fear: Analysis paralysis — fear of making the wrong decision.

R — Respond:

"Current technology is already mature — panels last 25–30 years. Is it worth waiting 3 years to gain 5% more efficiency, while paying $[MONTO_MENSUAL] per month to the utility?"
"The best solar technology is the one that's already producing energy today."

Objection 10: "I already have a generator" / "The generator is enough for me"

Real fear: They believe they've already solved the energy independence problem.

A — Acknowledge:

"Exactly — and that shows you already value energy independence."

R — Respond:

"How much do you spend on diesel per month? [Calculate together] Solar produces energy at $0.00 per kWh after the initial investment. Your generator costs you between $0.30 and $0.50 per kWh every time you run it."
"Many of our clients keep the generator as a backup and use solar as the main source. The best of both worlds."

Objection 11: "I don't have the capital available"

Real fear: Real cash constraint.

R — Respond:

"That's exactly what the PPA is for — you pay nothing upfront. We install the system, and you buy the energy it produces at a rate lower than the utility. Savings from month one with zero investment."
"Think of it as switching electricity providers — but one that charges you less and lives on your own roof."

Objection 12: "Why you and not [competitor name]?"

Real fear: They're doing their research — a good sign of genuine interest.

R — Respond:

"Excellent question. Two things set us apart: first, we are the only company focused 100% on commercial rooftops in Azuero — we're not based in Panama City sending technicians. We're here. Second, we don't just install: we give you a personalized ROI analysis with your real numbers, handle the net metering paperwork with ASEP, and monitor your system 24/7."
"What exactly did they offer you? I'd love to compare point by point with you."

Objection 13: "The ASEP paperwork is too complicated"

Real fear: Real fear of bureaucracy — completely valid in Panama.

A — Acknowledge:

"You're right — it can be a headache if you have to do it on your own."

R — Respond:

"That's why we handle all the paperwork: the ASEP application, coordination with the utility, municipal permits. You sign, we file. That service is included in our price."

Objection 14: "My roof won't work / it's too old"

Real fear: Could be reality or could be an unconfirmed assumption.

R — Respond:

"That's exactly what the free evaluation determines. Many times the roof works fine with minor reinforcements that cost very little. And if it genuinely isn't viable, we'll tell you honestly — we gain nothing by installing on a roof that doesn't work."

Additional Objections: Frequent in Azuero

Objection 15: "I don't have the money right now / the bank didn't approve my loan"

Real fear: Real financial constraint combined with frustration with the banking system.

A — Acknowledge:

"I understand — accessing financing isn't always straightforward, and that's frustrating."

R — Respond:

"That's exactly why we designed the PPA: you don't need capital or a bank loan. Zero upfront. Solaris installs the system, and you pay only for the energy you consume — at a rate of $0.14 to $0.17 per kWh, which is less than what you pay the utility today. You save from day one, with no debt."

Objection 16: "What happens when there's no sun, or at night?"

Real fear: They think they'll lose electricity when the sun isn't shining.

A — Acknowledge:

"That's a very logical question — and the answer surprises most people."

R — Respond:

"The system is connected to the electrical grid. During the day, your panels produce and supply energy directly to you; if you produce more than you consume, that surplus is credited through net metering. At night, the grid supplies electricity normally — just as always. You never lose power."
"On cloudy days, the panels keep producing — just at reduced capacity. Panama averages 5–6 kWh/m²/day of radiation, which already accounts for the cloudy days throughout the year."

Objection 17: "My neighbor / my friend says it's not worth it"

Real fear: Negative social pressure — they trust their network more than an unknown salesperson.

A — Acknowledge:

"It's important to listen to people you trust. That makes total sense."

E — Explore:

"Do you know whether your neighbor had direct experience with solar, or is it a general opinion?"

R — Respond:

"Sometimes another person's experience was with a different company, or with a system that was poorly sized for their consumption. Here's a concrete proposal: let's look at your specific numbers. Panama already has more than 170 MW installed with 6,000+ active users. What makes sense for one business may not for another — but with your real bill and your real roof, we'll show you whether it applies to you, with data, not opinions."

Objection 18: "I'd rather wait for solar prices to drop"

Real fear: Searching for the "perfect" moment — but the perfect moment never comes.

A — Acknowledge:

"It makes sense to want to optimize the investment."

R — Respond:

"Here's the real situation: electricity rates in Panama have risen more than 30% in the last 5 years — and that trend continues. Every month you wait, your bill goes up but solar doesn't come down proportionally."
"Also, Law 417, which reduces the effective cost of installation by 15–20%, has no guarantee of being maintained indefinitely. That benefit exists today."

Objection 19: "I already have a cheaper quote from another company"

Real fear: They want the best price — and suspect they can get it elsewhere.

A — Acknowledge:

"Good that you're comparing — it's your money and your business. That makes total sense."

E — Explore:

"May I see the quote? I'd like to understand exactly what it includes."

R — Respond (compare point by point):

"There are two key things to compare properly: first, the system size — a smaller system costs less but produces less energy, and your ROI is different. Second, what's included in the price: ASEP paperwork? 24/7 monitoring? 25-year panel warranty? Local Azuero team for service?"
"We won't lower our price — but we will make sure you're comparing apples to apples before you decide."

Objection 20: "Ecotourism is a trend — I'm not sure the image value is worth it"

Real fear: Not convinced that the image benefits justify the investment.

A — Acknowledge:

"I understand the doubt — there's a lot of sustainability marketing out there with no real substance behind it."

R — Respond:

"The financial ROI works completely independently of image: lower electricity costs, a 2.5 to 3.5-year payback period, 20+ years of savings. The image is an additional benefit that comes with the system."
"That said: ecotourism in Panama is growing 20% per year. If your guests can see that you generate your own clean energy, that's a real differentiator in the Azuero market."

⚠️ Common Mistakes with Objections

  1. Responding before listening. The response you give without listening addresses the wrong objection.
  2. Saying "but" after acknowledging. It cancels all the acknowledgment you just did. Use "and also" or simply continue with a pause.
  3. Discounting under pressure. The price doesn't go down — the value goes up. Add service, timeline, warranty.
  4. Taking the objection personally. An objection is a question disguised as resistance. It is a sign of interest.
  5. Launching data without first acknowledging the emotion. Data without empathy bounces off.
  6. Not closing after responding. Every response ends with a micro-close: *"Does that give you more peace of mind to move forward?"*
  7. Piling all responses when there are multiple objections. Resolve one at a time. Confirm it's resolved before moving to the next.

🎭 Role-Play: The Objection Marathon

Situation: Doña Carmen, owner of a farm in Pedasi, just heard the complete ROI presentation. The presentation went well — but when the price comes up she launches objections in a chain.

Partner A — Doña Carmen: Launch these four objections in order, one by one. Do not move to the next until the salesperson responds and asks whether the doubt is resolved:

  1. "That's a lot of money for me."
  2. "I'm not very trusting — a friend of mine had problems with a solar company."
  3. "What if I sell the farm someday?"
  4. "Let me think about it this week."

Partner B — Salesperson: Apply LAER to each objection. Confirm it is resolved before moving to the next. After the fourth objection, close with a next step with a specific date.

Time: 20 minutes per round. Switch roles. Discuss: which objection was hardest to handle and why?

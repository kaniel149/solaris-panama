## Part 2: The Three Payment Options

### Quick overview

| Option | Upfront investment | Who owns the system? | Savings |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|---------|
| **Direct Purchase** | High (client pays for the system) | The client from day 1 | Maximum in the long term |
| **PPA** | $0 | Solaris (for 15–20 years) | Immediate, moderate |
| **Lease** | $0 | Solaris (for 10–15 years) | Immediate, moderate |

---

### Option 1: Direct Purchase

#### How it works in plain language

The client pays for the complete system once. From that day, the system is theirs. The electricity their roof produces is free — forever.

#### Reference numbers

| System | Estimated cost | Monthly savings | Payback (with Law 417) |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| 80 kW | $80,000–$104,000 | ~$1,900/month | 2.5–3.5 years |
| 150 kW | $150,000–$195,000 | ~$3,600/month | 2.5–3.5 years |
| 300 kW | $300,000–$390,000 | ~$7,200/month | 2.5–3.5 years |

> Prices are based on $1.00–$1.30 per installed watt, including Law 417 benefits (equipment tariff exemption, ~10–15% savings on equipment costs).

#### 60-second pitch — Direct Purchase

> "The purchase option is this simple: you pay for the system once and it's yours forever. With the Law 417 benefits — which eliminate import taxes on the equipment — the payback drops to between 2.5 and 3.5 years. After that, you have more than 20 years of essentially free electricity. It's the option with the highest total return. An 80 kW system, for example, saves you around $1,900 per month."

#### Who this option is for

- Hotels and resorts with solid cash flow
- Property owners with available capital looking for the highest long-term ROI
- Businesses that want complete energy independence

---

### Option 2: PPA (Power Purchase Agreement)

#### How it works in plain language

Solaris installs the system on the client's roof. Solaris retains ownership of the system. The client buys the electricity that system produces at a fixed rate, lower than the grid. The client pays less from the first month — without investing a single cent.

#### Reference numbers

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Client's upfront investment | **$0** |
| PPA rate the client pays | $0.14–$0.17/kWh |
| Grid rate they pay today | ~$0.20/kWh |
| Savings from the first month | 15–30% on solar electricity |
| Contract duration | 15–20 years |
| Annual PPA rate increase | 1–2%/year |
| Historical annual grid rate increase | 3–5%/year |
| At the end of the contract | The client can buy the system, renew, or have it removed |

#### For an 80 kW system (example)

- Immediate monthly savings: ~$450–$700
- Cumulative savings over 20 years: ~$100,000–$160,000
- Risk to the client: none

#### 60-second pitch — PPA

> "Imagine switching electricity providers — except the new provider lives on your own roof. With the PPA, Solaris installs the panels at no cost to you. You buy the energy they produce at $0.14–$0.17 per kWh, instead of the $0.20 you pay the utility today. You save between 15 and 30% from the first month, with zero upfront investment. The contract lasts 15 to 20 years. We maintain the system. At the end, you can buy it."

#### Who this option is for

- Farms and small businesses with limited budgets
- Businesses that prefer operating expenses (OPEX) over capital investment (CAPEX)
- Property owners who want immediate savings without financial risk
- Companies where approving a large investment takes time

---

### Option 3: Lease

#### How it works in plain language

The client pays a fixed monthly fee to use the system. That fee is lower than what they currently spend on electricity, so they still save from the first month. The system remains Solaris's during the contract.

#### Reference numbers (80 kW system)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Upfront investment | **$0** |
| Monthly lease payment | $800–$1,200/month |
| Value of energy produced (at grid rate) | ~$1,900/month |
| Net monthly savings | ~$700–$1,100/month |
| Contract duration | 10–15 years |
| At the end of the contract | Buy for $1, renew, or have the system removed |

#### 60-second pitch — Lease

> "With the lease, you pay a fixed monthly fee — let's say $1,000 per month — and the system produces electricity equivalent to $1,900 per month at grid rates. The difference, almost $900, is your net savings from month one. No upfront investment, no surprise fee increases, and you know exactly what you're going to pay every month. At the end of the contract, you can buy the system for $1 or return it to us."

#### Who this option is for

- Companies that want predictable costs and simple accounting
- Businesses where the owner doesn't want to assume ownership of the system
- Clients who prefer quick decisions without complex financial analysis

## Part 3: Which Option to Recommend for Each Client

### Quick decision guide

| Client type | Recommended option | Primary reason |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Hotel / Resort (owner with capital)** | Direct Purchase | Cash flow, maximum ROI, eco-branding |
| **Hotel / Resort (cautious owner)** | PPA | $0 upfront, starts saving immediately |
| **Supermarket (corporate chain)** | PPA | Slow CAPEX approval process; with PPA the local GM can say yes faster |
| **Dairy farm / agro-industrial** | PPA or Purchase | If they have capital: Purchase. If not: PPA for the immediate savings |
| **Surf camp / boutique lodge** | PPA or Lease | Small property owners, $0 upfront is the easiest argument |
| **Large farm with cash flow** | Direct Purchase | Maximum return, no third parties involved long term |

### The general presentation rule

**Always present Direct Purchase first.** Anchor the system's value ($80,000–$300,000 depending on size) before showing the no-upfront-cost alternatives. That way the client understands the real value of what we're giving them "for free" with the PPA or Lease.

## Part 4: The ROI Data — What You Need to Collect

### The 3 essential data points

You don't calculate the ROI — that is the manager's and technical team's job. Your job is to bring these three data points from every visit:

| Data point | How to get it | Why it matters |
|------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Monthly electricity bill ($)** | Ask for a photo of the bill or the amount | Base of the savings calculation |
| **Monthly consumption in kWh** | It's on the same bill | Determines the size of the system |
| **Roof area (m²)** | Visual estimate + satellite (manager confirms) | Limits the maximum system size |

### Quick field estimation formula (reference only — do not share with client)

```
Usable roof area ≈ 60–70% of total area
Estimated system (kW) = Usable area (m²) × 0.15
Monthly production (kWh) = System (kW) × 5.0 × 30 × 0.85
Estimated monthly savings ($) = Production (kWh) × $0.20
```

### Real-world examples to memorize

| Prospect type | Usable roof | System | Estimated monthly savings |
|--------------|------------|--------|--------------------------|
| Mid-size hotel (400 m² usable) | 400 m² | 60–80 kW | $1,400–$1,900 |
| Supermarket (1,500 m² usable) | 1,500 m² | 225 kW | ~$5,740 |
| Dairy farm (400 m² shed) | 400 m² | 60 kW | ~$1,530 |

> These are reference ranges. **The final number is always presented by the manager in the formal proposal.**

## Part 5: The Golden Rule — The Manager Approves Prices

This rule is not optional. It is Solaris Panama policy and it protects both the client and the salesperson.

### Why does this rule exist?

1. **Prices depend on technical variables** you don't have in the field (roof type, slope, distance to the inverter, existing electrical capacity).
2. **PPA and Lease options have contractual terms** that only the manager can formally commit to.
3. **A price given incorrectly creates expectations that are impossible to meet** — and that destroys trust.

### What you CAN tell the client about prices

- **Reference ranges** from the sales materials: "An 80 kW system can be between $80,000 and $104,000 installed, depending on the roof characteristics."
- **The reference PPA rate:** "The PPA rate is between $0.14 and $0.17 per kWh, versus the $0.20 you pay today."
- **The payback range:** "With the Law 417 benefits, the payback is typically between 2.5 and 4.5 years."

### What you should NEVER tell the client

- An exact installation price without a formal proposal from the manager.
- That you can offer discounts or adjust the PPA rate.
- That you can commit to installation dates without confirmation from the team.

### Transition phrase when the client asks about price

> "I want to be straight with you: to give you an exact number I need the manager to review the specific characteristics of your roof and your consumption. What I can tell you is that a system of this size typically falls in the range of [X]–[Y]. Let's coordinate a free technical visit and in less than a week you'll have the complete proposal in hand."

## 📞 Scripts and Key Phrases

### To present the 3 options in a single conversation

> "Mr. [NOMBRE], we have three ways of working together. The first is the direct purchase: you invest in the system, it's yours from day one, and after [PAYBACK] years the electricity is essentially free. The second is the PPA: we install at no cost to you, and you only pay us for the electricity you use — at a rate lower than the grid. The third is the lease: a fixed monthly payment, lower than your current bill, with no upfront investment. Which of the three fits best with how you manage your finances?"

### To recommend PPA to a client with a tight budget

> "Given what you've told me about your cash flow, I'd recommend the PPA. No upfront investment. You start saving from the first month — between 15 and 30% of your solar bill. And if at some point you want to buy the system, you have that option at the end of the contract."

### To recommend Purchase to a hotel with capital

> "With the capital you mentioned, the direct purchase is clearly the best option for you. After [PAYBACK] years — taking advantage of the Law 417 benefits — you have essentially free electricity for 20 more years. The difference in total savings versus the PPA can be hundreds of thousands of dollars."

### To explain the Lease

> "The lease is the simplest option of all. You pay $[MONTO] fixed per month, no surprises. The system produces $[VALOR] in electricity per month. The difference is your net savings. No investment, no risk, no complications."

### When the client asks if they can negotiate the price

> "I completely understand. What I can tell you is that instead of lowering the price, what we do is add value — for example, extending the monitoring period or including additional maintenance visits. But to talk about the exact terms, the manager needs to confirm that. Can I set up a call this week?"

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes in This Module

1. **Offering the wrong option without asking first.** Before recommending PPA or Purchase, ask: "Do you prefer to make an upfront investment or start with $0?" The client's answer guides you.

2. **Promising exact prices.** If the client insists, give ranges ("between $80,000 and $104,000") and explain that the exact number depends on the technical visit.

3. **Presenting only one option.** Always present all three — even if you know which one fits best. A client who knows all three options makes their decision with more confidence.

4. **Confusing PPA with Lease.** In the PPA the client pays per kWh produced (variable). In the Lease they pay a fixed monthly fee. They are different contracts with different implications.

5. **Not collecting the bill on the first visit.** Without that data, the manager cannot prepare the proposal. If the client doesn't have the bill on hand, ask them to send a photo by WhatsApp that same day.

6. **Giving verbal discounts.** Even to close, never promise a lower price without manager authorization. That verbal price can become an expectation that's impossible to honor.

**Situation:** You are meeting with Ms. [NOMBRE], the owner of a mid-size supermarket in Chitré. You've just shown her the data: roof of ~1,200 m², bill of $5,800/month. She says: "I'm interested, but the company doesn't have capital for a large investment right now. Do you have any alternative?"

1. Present the PPA as the first alternative (with the reference rate and estimated savings).

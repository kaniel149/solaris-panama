## Theory: The Cold B2B Lead vs. the Hot Lead

In Module 3 we worked with people who already raised their hand — they filled out the form. In this module we learn how to open businesses that have never heard of Solaris Panama.

**The key difference:**

| Dimension | Hot Lead | Cold B2B Lead |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Initial interest | Already exists | You have to create it |
| Sales cycle length | Days to weeks | Weeks to months |
| First objective | Book a 15-min call | Create curiosity, don't sell |
| Dominant channel | WhatsApp | WhatsApp + Email + In-person visit |
| Expected resistance | Low | High (gatekeepers, "not interested") |

**Why we pursue B2B:** Commercial clients — hotels, supermarkets, large farms — have bills of $1,500/month or more, roofs of 400 m²+, and a system can be worth $18,000-$100,000+ USD. One B2B client is equivalent to 10-15 residential clients.

### The Three Priority Segments in Azuero

**Tier 1 — Maximum priority:**
- **Hotels and resorts** (especially with expat owners — Canadians, Americans, Europeans): AC running 24/7, high bill, owner familiar with solar, motivated by ROI and eco-branding.
- **Supermarkets:** Enormous flat roofs, constant refrigeration, corporate pressure to reduce costs.

**Tier 2 — High priority:**
- **Large farms (agro-industrial):** Sheds with large roofs, water pumping and milking as constant loads, relationship-first culture.

**Critical Azuero insight:** Across the entire Azuero Peninsula **there is no competitor focused on commercial rooftop solar**. You are not displacing a competitor — you are creating the conversation from scratch. The objection is not "why you?" but "why solar?, why now?"

### How to Get Past the Receptionist

Many hotels and supermarkets have a receptionist or secretary before you can reach the owner or manager. Your goal is to get to the decision-maker.

**Script for the receptionist:**

> "Good morning, could you connect me with the general manager or property owner? My name is [AGENT] from Solaris Panama — we're calling about an energy savings assessment for the property. It's a 3-minute call."

**Rules when speaking with the receptionist:**
- Be brief and direct. Don't explain solar.
- Ask for a name: "Who can I speak with about operational cost matters?"
- If they say "send an email": ask for the manager's direct email, not the generic info@ address.
- If they say "they're not available": "What time are they usually in? I'll call then."
- **Never lie** about who you are or the reason for the call.

---

### In-Person Visit — Protocol

For farms and smaller hotels, a drop-in in-person visit is a valid tactic. Follow this protocol:

1. **Introduce yourself at the reception or entrance:** "Good morning, I'm [AGENT] from Solaris Panama. I was in the area and wanted to leave some information about reducing electricity costs for the property. Is the owner or manager available?"
2. **If they're available:** Three minutes maximum — present the value, ask for 15 minutes tomorrow.
3. **If they're not in:** Leave your card with a handwritten note. Send a WhatsApp that afternoon: "Good morning [NOMBRE], I stopped by [HOTEL] today and left my card with the front desk. I wanted to introduce myself — we specialize in solar for hotels in Azuero. Could we talk for 10 minutes this week?"

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

1. **Talking about prices in the first contact.** The first contact is to create curiosity. If you quote a price before understanding the context, you give a number and the prospect rejects it without context. Don't mention prices at T+0.

2. **Using the same tone for everyone.** The Canadian expat and the Panamanian farm owner are completely different people. Use informal English for expats, formal Spanish for corporate accounts, approachable language for farms.

3. **Skipping the email sequence for supermarkets.** Supermarkets are corporate. They don't make decisions by WhatsApp. The formal email is the first step — the in-person visit is the second.

4. **Not asking who the decision-maker is from the start.** If you're speaking with the floor manager but not the owner or general manager, you're wasting time. Always confirm: "Are you the final decision-maker on investment matters for the property?"

5. **Mentioning the competition.** In Azuero there are no rooftop solar competitors — don't mention them, even favorably. The conversation is "why solar?" not "why us?"

6. **Visiting during peak hours.** A hotel at 11 AM on a Saturday is in the worst possible moment for a drop-in. Visit Tuesday through Thursday, before 10 AM or between 2-4 PM.

7. **Not recording the receptionist's name.** If the receptionist helped you, note their name. The next time you call: "Good morning [NOMBRE DE RECEPCIONISTA], this is [AGENT] from Solaris Panama — we spoke last week. Is [GERENTE] available?" It builds trust.

## 🎭 Role-Play

**Exercise: The Expat Hotel**

**Context:** Casa Lajagua is a boutique hotel in Pedasi. The owner is Canadian, his name is [NOMBRE]. You found his WhatsApp number on the hotel's Facebook page.

**Characters:**
- **Agent (you):** Sends the first WhatsApp in English (Day 1).
- **[NOMBRE] (colleague):** Responds after 2 days with: "Hi, yes I've thought about solar. How much does it cost?"

**Instructions for the agent:**
1. Send Touch 1 in English (you may read it from the script)
2. When [NOMBRE] asks about price, do NOT give an immediate price — follow the process: "Great question — to give you real numbers I'd need to know your monthly electricity bill and roof area. Can we jump on a 15-min call this week?"
3. Close with two time slot options

**Goal:** Book the 15-minute call without giving a price until you have context.

**Evaluation checklist:**
- Was the first message in English? (Yes / No)
- Did they avoid giving a price before getting the bill? (Yes / No)
- Did they offer two time slot options? (Yes / No)
- Did they mention Law 417 at some point? (Yes / No)
